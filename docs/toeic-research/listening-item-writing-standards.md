# Listening-Assessment Item-Writing Standards

*Psychometric synthesis for WordWar listening MCQ design. Drawn from Haladyna & Downing's item-writing taxonomy, the AERA/APA/NCME Standards (2014), Buck's listening-testing research, Bachman & Palmer's test usefulness framework, and medical-education distractor-functionality studies.*

---

## Executive Summary

A high-quality listening multiple-choice item is a measurement instrument, not a puzzle. Its job is to produce **variance that maps onto the listening construct** (parsing connected speech, retaining propositions, drawing inferences) and **suppress variance from anything else** — what Messick called *construct-irrelevant variance* (CIV). Three findings dominate the literature:

1. **Most MCQ defects come from a small number of avoidable flaws.** Haladyna, Downing & Rodriguez (2002) consolidated 31 item-writing rules from 27 measurement textbooks; later empirical work (Downing 2002, Tarrant et al. 2009) showed that violating even one or two of these flaws materially inflates or deflates scores.
2. **Distractor quantity is overrated; distractor plausibility is everything.** Empirical analyses of thousands of MCQs find that 30-50% of distractors are non-functional (chosen by <5% of test-takers). Rodriguez's meta-analysis concludes that **3 well-functioning options beat 4 options with a dud** — there is no psychometric magic in "1 correct + 3 distractors."
3. **In listening, the dominant CIV source is verbatim audio-script matching.** Buck (1991, 2001) demonstrated that test-takers exploit lexical overlap between the audio and the printed options; items that allow this measure auditory word-spotting, not comprehension.

The "1+1+1+1" doctrine (1 correct + 1 same-category + 1 partial-truth + 1 obvious-miss) **is not a recognized psychometric standard**. It encodes the obvious-miss as a deliberately non-functional distractor, which empirically reduces effective option count to 3 and signals the answer to testwise candidates. A defensible alternative is **"1 + 3 plausible-but-wrong-for-different-reasons"**, where each distractor maps to a documented listening-comprehension barrier (mishearing, partial parse, plausible inference, schema-driven guess).

## Top 10 Item-Writing Rules for Listening MCQs

Adapted from Haladyna, Downing & Rodriguez (2002) and the NBME Item-Writing Guide, filtered for listening-specific applicability.

1. **Each item should assess one clearly specified objective.** Tag every item with a construct sub-skill (gist / detail / inference / function / vocabulary-in-context). If you cannot name the sub-skill, the item is untestable.
2. **The stem should be self-contained and answerable without reading the options.** A native listener should be able to answer from memory of the audio. Options should *confirm*, not *constitute*, the question.
3. **Avoid verbatim lexical overlap between the correct option and the audio script.** Paraphrase the key proposition. Verbatim matches let candidates score via auditory word-spotting (Buck 2001) — construct-irrelevant.
4. **Make all options grammatically parallel and homogeneous in content.** Mixed grammar (one option starts with a verb, three with nouns) signals the odd-one-out — a classic Haladyna flaw.
5. **Keep option length roughly equal.** Empirical studies of TOEFL and medical exams show the longest option is the key 60-70% of the time when writers over-qualify the right answer. Trim or pad to within ±20% length.
6. **Avoid "all of the above" and "none of the above."** Haladyna Rule 22. Both inflate guessing variance and reward partial knowledge / testwiseness. Forbidden in NBME and most standardized programs.
7. **No negative stems unless the negative is in bold and load-bearing.** "Which is NOT mentioned" doubles cognitive load and confounds listening skill with reading-instruction parsing.
8. **Position the key randomly across items.** Roughly 25% A / 25% B / 25% C / 25% D across the form. Humans default to B/C — explicit randomization prevents positional bias.
9. **Each distractor should be defensibly wrong for a stated reason.** Document in item metadata: "Distractor B = phonological confusion with X"; "Distractor C = schema-driven inference not supported by audio." This is the bedrock of distractor analysis and revision.
10. **Pilot every item and retire non-functioning distractors.** A distractor chosen by <5% of low-scorers is dead weight; merge or rewrite. This is the standard distractor-efficiency analysis (Tarrant et al. 2009).

## Distractor Design Heuristics

### Why "1+1+1+1" (obvious-miss included) is psychometrically weak

| Issue | Evidence |
|---|---|
| The obvious-miss is non-functional. | 30-50% of distractors in field studies are eliminated by <5% of candidates (Tarrant 2009; Haladyna & Downing 1989). |
| It shortens effective option count to 3. | Rodriguez's meta-analysis: 3-option items perform as reliably as 4-option items *when distractors function*. So you gain no reliability and lose one slot of diagnostic signal. |
| It signals the answer via contrast. | Testwise candidates eliminate the obvious-miss instantly, reducing the item to a 3-way choice with one same-category distractor — i.e., a coin-flip with a hint. |

### Recommended frame: "4 plausible, 4 different failure modes"

Each distractor should encode a **specific listening barrier** documented in cognitive-diagnostic listening research (Bachman 1990; Buck 2001; recent CDA studies in PMC):

| Distractor type | What it diagnoses | Example trigger |
|---|---|---|
| **Phonological confusion** | Bottom-up decoding failure | Audio: "fifteen" → distractor: "fifty" |
| **Local-detail substitution** | Caught a word, missed scope | Audio: "she rejected the offer" → distractor uses the offer's price |
| **Schema-driven inference** | Over-extrapolated from world knowledge | Audio about a delayed flight → distractor mentions "weather" though audio said "mechanical" |
| **Partial parse / mid-utterance stop** | Stopped processing too early | Audio: "X, but actually Y" → distractor reflects X |

This is essentially the **5Ps typology** (plausible, peripheral, polyconceptual, prejudicial, pragmatic) proposed in recent EFL MCQ literature, pared to four operational categories.

## Answer-Leak Prevention Checklist

Run every item through this before piloting.

- [ ] **Length parity.** Longest option ≤ 1.25× shortest. Strip qualifiers ("usually," "in most cases") from the key if other options lack them.
- [ ] **No verbatim audio lift.** Run a diff: any 4+ word string that appears in both the audio script and a single option = rewrite.
- [ ] **No grammatical tells.** All options agree with the stem in number, tense, article, preposition.
- [ ] **No convergence cue.** If 3 of 4 options share a word or concept, the 4th is dead — flip the structure.
- [ ] **No absolute-vs-hedged contrast.** Avoid one option using "always/never" while others use "often/sometimes" — the hedged one is almost always the key.
- [ ] **No clang associations.** Don't repeat the stem's distinctive word in only one option.
- [ ] **Process-of-elimination test.** Cover the audio and the correct answer. Can a smart non-listener pick the key from the stem + options alone? If yes, the item leaks.
- [ ] **Key-balance check at form level.** A/B/C/D each within 20-30% across the section.
- [ ] **Negative-stem audit.** If "NOT," "EXCEPT," or "LEAST" appears, it must be visually emphasized and there must be no double-negation.
- [ ] **Distractor rationale on file.** Each wrong option has a one-line "why a real test-taker would pick this."

## Within-Lesson Variety Patterns

When 10-12 items probe the same passage (common in TOEFL iBT lectures, Cambridge Main Suite Part 3-4, and TOEIC Part 3-4 sets), the construct demands **sub-skill rotation**. Buck (2001) and the TOEFL iBT framework operationalize six listening sub-skills; a well-designed set hits at least four:

| Sub-skill | What it tests | Sample stem template |
|---|---|---|
| **Gist (main idea)** | Top-down synthesis | "What is the speaker mainly discussing?" |
| **Detail** | Local fact retention | "How much did X cost?" |
| **Inference** | Indirect meaning | "What does the speaker imply about Y?" |
| **Function / pragmatic** | Speech-act recognition | "Why does the speaker say 'I see'?" |
| **Vocabulary-in-context** | Lexical inference | "What does the speaker mean by Z?" |
| **Connecting content / organization** | Discourse-level structure | "How is the lecture organized?" / "What will the speaker discuss next?" |

**Heuristic for an N-item set:** at minimum, 1 gist + 1 inference + 1 function + (N-3) details, with no two items sharing the same sub-skill back-to-back. This mirrors the TOEFL iBT blueprint and avoids construct under-representation (Bachman & Palmer 1996).

## Cross-Question Similarity Detection Heuristic

To detect the "Q1 and Q5 both ask 'what kind of cat'" pathology, apply three filters in order.

1. **Sub-skill fingerprint match.** If two items in the same set carry the same sub-skill tag *and* target the same referent in the audio (same noun phrase, same speaker turn), they are duplicates. Rewrite one to target a different referent or sub-skill.

2. **Option-set Jaccard similarity.** Treat each item's 4 options as a bag of content words. Compute Jaccard overlap between every item pair in the set. **Pairs with Jaccard ≥ 0.4 are suspect; ≥ 0.6 are duplicates.** Cheap, deterministic, runnable in CI.

3. **Stem-noun overlap.** Extract the head noun of each stem ("the cat," "the cat's owner"). Two items sharing the same head noun should test *different sub-skills* (e.g., one detail, one inference). If sub-skills also match, collapse or rewrite.

**Operational rule for an item-writer:** before adding item N to a set, list the (referent, sub-skill) pair for items 1 to N-1. Item N's pair must be novel on at least one axis.
